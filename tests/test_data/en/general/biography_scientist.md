## Preface

Zu Chongzhi was an outstanding mathematician, astronomer, and inventor during the Northern and Southern Dynasties of China. He achieved remarkable accomplishments in multiple fields including mathematics, astronomical calendar, and mechanical manufacturing, standing as a monument in the history of ancient Chinese science.

This book aims to document the extraordinary life of Zu Chongzhi, showcasing his steadfast pursuit of science and great contributions to Chinese civilization.

## Chapter 1: Early Years (429-450)

### 1.1 Birth and Family

In 429 (the sixth year of the Yuanjia era under Emperor Wen of Liu Song), Zu Chongzhi was born in **Fanyang Commandery Qiuxian** (now Laishui County, Hebei Province) into an official family.

His great-grandfather **Zu Taizhi** served as Imperial Secretary and Guanglu Doctor during the Eastern Jin Dynasty. His grandfather **Zu Chang** held the position of Grand Master of Architecture (an official overseeing civil engineering) during the Liu Song Dynasty. His father **Zu Shuo Zhi** served as a court official. In this family environment, Zu Chongzhi received excellent education from an early age and developed a strong interest in science and technology.

### 1.2 Youth and Love of Learning

Zu Chongzhi was studious from childhood, reading extensively, with a particular fondness for books on mathematics, astronomy, and mechanical manufacturing.

Historical records describe Zu Chongzhi as "having extraordinary intelligence and thinking ability, studying ancient classics from youth."

### 1.3 Entering Officialdom

Around 450, Zu Chongzhi entered official service, serving as an official historian in Southern Xuzhou (now Zhenjiang, Jiangsu), later becoming a military officer in the duke's residence. Although in officialdom, he never abandoned his pursuit of scientific research.

## Chapter 2: Mathematical Achievements (450-470)

### 2.1 Calculation of Pi

Zu Chongzhi's most outstanding contribution to mathematics was the precise calculation of pi (π).

After long and arduous research, Zu Chongzhi determined:
- **Ying Shu (Upper Limit)**: 3.1415927
- **Nü Shu (Lower Limit)**: 3.1415926

That is, the value of pi lies between **3.1415926 and 3.1415927**, accurate to the 7th decimal place. This achievement preceded Western mathematicians by **over a thousand years**!

For convenience in calculation, Zu Chongzhi also provided two fractional approximations:
- **Yuelü (Approximate Ratio)**: 22/7 (≈3.1429)
- **Milü (Precise Ratio)**: 355/113 (≈3.1415929)

Among them, the precise ratio 355/113 is the best approximate fraction with numerator and denominator under 1000, also leading the world by over a thousand years.

### 2.2 "Zhuishu"

Zu Chongzhi compiled his mathematical research into a work called **"Zhuishu"** (Mathematical Treatise).

"Zhuishu" was profound in content, known as a "classic of mathematical studies," and later became one of the textbooks for the mathematics department of the Imperial Academy during the Tang Dynasty. Unfortunately, this precious mathematical work was lost during the Northern Song Dynasty.

## Chapter 3: Astronomical Calendar (450-464)

### 3.1 Discovering Calendar Problems

While researching astronomical calendars, Zu Chongzhi found numerous errors in the prevailing **"Yuanjia Calendar"**.

The Yuanjia Calendar was formulated by astronomer He Chengtian. Although more precise than previous calendars, it still had deficiencies. After years of observation and research, Zu Chongzhi pointed out multiple flaws in the Yuanjia Calendar.

### 3.2 Creating the "Daming Calendar"

In 462 (the sixth year of the Daming era under Emperor Xiaowu of Liu Song), Zu Chongzhi compiled a new calendar — the **"Daming Calendar"**.

The main innovations of the Daming Calendar include:
1. **Introduction of Precession**: A major breakthrough in Chinese calendar history, distinguishing between tropical year and sidereal year
2. **Improved Lunisolar Cycle**: Changed from the old calendar's 19-year-7-intercalation to 391-year-144-intercalation, more precise
3. **Accurate Determination of Nodal Month**: Calculated the nodal month as 27.21223 days, differing minimally from the modern measurement of 27.21222 days
4. **Precise Determination of Five Planets' Cycles**: Conducted precise measurements of the orbital periods of the five planets: Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn
