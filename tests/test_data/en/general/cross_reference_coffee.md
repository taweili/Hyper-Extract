# Coffee

**Beverage Type**: Caffeinated Drink
**Main Ingredients**: Coffee Beans, Water
**Common Consumption Scenarios**: Breakfast, Socializing, Work, Relaxation

---

## Overview

**Coffee** is a beverage made primarily from roasted coffee beans, and is one of the most common hot drinks in modern urban life. It can be consumed alone or combined with milk, syrup, cream, and other ingredients to create beverages with distinctly different flavors. Because coffee has stimulating effects, people typically view it as a common choice for morning workdays, afternoon breaks, and social gatherings.

In daily experience, coffee is frequently discussed alongside **tea**, as both are widely popular beverages containing stimulating ingredients, but differ significantly in flavor, preparation methods, and consumption culture. To understand the stimulating effects of coffee from an ingredient perspective, one typically needs to further consult "**Caffeine**."

---

## Ingredients and Names

The ingredients for coffee come from the seeds of the coffee tree, commonly known as coffee beans. After harvesting, coffee beans undergo processing, drying, roasting, and grinding before entering the brewing stage. The degree of roasting significantly affects bitterness, acidity, and aroma. Therefore, many articles introducing coffee mention "**roasting**" and "**grinding**."

## Historical Spread

Coffee was originally not a global daily beverage; its spread underwent a lengthy historical process. With the expansion of trade networks, the formation of colonial plantation systems, and the development of urban public life, coffee gradually transformed from a regional beverage into a cross-cultural consumer product. In modern times, coffee is not merely a drink but also part of commercial spaces, reading, writing, and social activities.

In European urban history and modern public culture studies, coffee is frequently discussed alongside "**cafés**" because many intellectual exchanges, literary activities, and civic gatherings were related to café spaces. Regarding this spatial form and its social functions, see "café."

## Preparation Methods

Coffee flavor is influenced not only by bean variety but also by brewing methods. Common methods in home environments include drip brewing, immersion, French press, and capsule machine brewing; in professional stores, **espresso** based on high-pressure extraction is commonly seen. Espresso can be consumed alone or used as a base for other beverages, which is why many coffee knowledge entries list "espresso" as a related entry.

Adding steamed milk to espresso typically produces a **latte**; varying proportions of milk and milk foam may create a **cappuccino**. If introducing coffee from the perspective of home preparation and flavor control, text often shifts toward "**pour-over coffee**" because it better demonstrates the impact of water temperature, grind size, and pouring methods on flavor.

Therefore, in beverage classification, the general "coffee" entry often maintains stable cross-reference relationships with entries like "espresso," "latte," "cappuccino," and "pour-over coffee."

## Flavor and Components

Coffee flavor is typically composed of acidity, bitterness, sweetness, body, and aroma layers. Different origins, roasting methods, and extraction techniques can present the same beans with noticeably different tastes. For ordinary consumers, the most easily perceived component influence is still the **caffeine** content. Regarding coffee's effects on alertness, sleep, and heart rate, discussions typically do not expand within the "coffee" entry but refer to "caffeine."

In beverage comparisons, coffee is also frequently contrasted with **cocoa** or tea. Compared to cocoa, coffee typically emphasizes roasted aroma and bitterness; compared to tea, coffee in modern consumption contexts is more often associated with takeaway, stores, and office scenarios. For such comparative reading, see "tea" and "cocoa."

## Daily Culture

In many countries and regions, coffee has long exceeded the beverage itself to become part of daily rhythm. People buy coffee during commutes, briefly stop by during meeting breaks, and treat weekend store visits and tasting new beans as leisure activities. With the proliferation of chain brands, independent stores, and home brewing equipment, coffee consumption has formed very rich layers. This culture also maintains a stable connection between "coffee" and "café." For many readers, understanding coffee is not just understanding a liquid, but understanding an urban experience unfolding around space, socializing, and consumption patterns.

---

## Health and Controversy

Regarding whether coffee is healthy, there have long been differing viewpoints. Proponents typically emphasize the benefits of moderate consumption for alertness and maintaining concentration; critics worry about high-frequency intake affecting sleep, anxiety levels, or cardiovascular status. Since most of these discussions are directly related to caffeine intake, relevant materials often further direct discussions toward "caffeine."

Additionally, differences between instant coffee, chain store beverages, and specialty coffee are frequently discussed by consumers and media. The focus of controversy usually lies not in "whether coffee is coffee," but in flavor standards, added ingredients, pricing systems, and consumption experiences.
