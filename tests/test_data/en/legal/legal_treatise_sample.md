# Principles of Fault Determination in Civil Tort Liability — Centered on Article 1165 of the Civil Code

## Chapter 1: Basic Theory of Tort Liability Law

### 1.1 Jurisprudential Basis of Fault Liability Principle

The fault liability principle is the core attribution principle of civil tort liability. Its jurisprudential basis lies in:
- **Principle of Individual Responsibility**: Actors are only responsible for their own wrongful acts
- **Preventive Function**: Through sanctions against wrongful acts, preventing the occurrence of improper behavior
- **Freedom Guarantee**: Actors enjoy freedom of action without violating laws

### 1.2 Concept and Types of Fault

**Fault** includes two forms: intent and negligence:

**Intent**: The psychological state of an actor who foresees the occurrence of damage and hopes or allows the result to occur. Intent can be divided into:
- Direct intent: hoping for the occurrence of damage
- Indirect intent: allowing the occurrence of damage

**Negligence**: The psychological state where an actor should have foreseen the occurrence of damage but failed to foresee it due to carelessness, or had foreseen it but believed it could be avoided. Negligence can be divided into:
- Ordinary negligence: duty of care of an ordinary person
- Gross negligence: clearly exceeding the degree of care of an ordinary person

## Chapter 2: Normative Interpretation of Article 1165 of the Civil Code

### 2.1 Text Content

> Article 1165 of the Civil Code of the People's Republic of China: An actor who causes damage to another person's civil rights and interests due to fault shall bear tort liability.
> If fault is presumed against an actor according to law, and the actor cannot prove the absence of fault, they shall bear tort liability.

### 2.2 Normative Structure

This article adopts the legislative model of a general clause, containing the following elements:
1. **Act**: An act that infringes upon civil rights and interests
2. **Fault**: Intent or negligence in the actor's subjectivity
3. **Damage**: The occurrence of actual damage
4. **Causal Relationship**: Existence of a causal relationship between the act and the damage

### 2.3 Burden of Proof

According to the "whoever claims must prove" principle, the plaintiff must prove:
- The defendant committed a tortious act
- The defendant had fault
- The plaintiff suffered damage
- There is a causal relationship between the act and the damage

Exception: When the law presumes fault, the defendant must prove the absence of their fault (e.g., product liability, medical malpractice liability).

## Chapter 3: Standards for Fault Determination in Case Law

### 3.1 Fault Determination in Cases of Security Obligation

**Guiding Case 140**: In public places or roads, excavation, repair, or installation of underground facilities causing damage to others, if the constructor cannot prove that obvious signs have been set up and safety measures have been taken, they shall bear tort liability.

This case establishes the "result obligation" nature of the security obligation — as long as damage occurs and obligation fulfillment cannot be proven, fault is presumed.

### 3.2 Fault Determination in Medical Malpractice Liability

**Guiding Case 142**: If a patient suffers damage during diagnosis and treatment activities, and the medical institution and its medical personnel have fault, the medical institution shall bear compensation liability. The determination of medical fault shall be based on the medical standard at that time.

Here, "medical standard at that time" includes:
- Duty of care stipulated by diagnosis and treatment norms
- Medical consensus at that time
- The conditions and capabilities of the medical institution itself

### 3.3 Fault Presumption in High-Risk Liability

According to Article 1239 of the Civil Code, operators engaged in high-altitude, high-pressure, underground excavation activities or using high-speed rail transportation tools causing damage to others shall bear tort liability; however, if they can prove that the damage was caused by the victim's intentional act or force majeure, they shall not bear liability.

## Chapter 4: Doctrinal Debates on Fault Determination

### 4.1 Subjective Fault Theory vs. Objective Fault Theory

**Subjective Fault Theory**: Fault is the internal psychological state of an actor, constituting moral condemnation.
